Knitting Lessons

Six step-by-step lessons taking you from complete beginner to confident knitter. Click any lesson to expand it.

What You Need to Get Started

Materials

Before your first stitch, gather these basics.

🪡
Knitting Needles

1 set of knitting needles.

🧶
Yarn

At least a mini skein of yarn.

✂️
Scissors

Any scissors for cutting yarn.

🛡️
Point Protectors

Optional but recommended, to keep stitches from slipping off the needle when you are not knitting.

Tip: If you are using a combination of knit and purl stitches, keep a pencil and paper handy to track your rows.
2
Casting On

What is Casting On?

Casting on creates the first row of loops on your needle. These loops become the foundation of your knitting.

How to Cast On

  1. Tie a slip knot by pinching a crossed loop, leaving 1-2 inches on the end and pulling the yarn attached to the ball over the end and through the loop, creating a new loop.
  2. Put the new loop on one needle and put that needle in your non-dominant hand.
  3. Stick your dominant needle upwards into the top loop to the back and on the opposite side of the knot.
  4. Wrap your dominant needle around the yarn attached to the ball once while it is wrapped around your non-dominant pointer finger behind your project.
  5. Take the new loop out of the loop you stuck your dominant needle through.
  6. Slip the bottom of the new loop onto your non-dominant needle.
  7. Repeat steps 3–6 until you reach your desired width.
Tip: To keep your tension just right, you should feel the yarn gently tugging on your pointer finger. Most importantly, hold the needles with the tips up.
3
The Knit Stitch

The Foundation of Knitting

The knit stitch (abbreviated k in patterns) is the most fundamental knitting movement. All the knit stitch is, is casting-on, but instead of slipping the new loop onto your non-dominant needle, slip the top loop off your non-dominant needle.

How to Knit

  1. Stick your dominant needle upwards into the top loop to the back and on the opposite side of the knot.
  2. Wrap your dominant needle around the yarn attached to the ball once while it is wrapped around your non-dominant pointer finger behind your project.
  3. Take the new loop out of the loop you stuck your dominant needle through.
  4. Instead of slipping the new loop onto your non-dominant needle, slip the top loop off your non-dominant needle.
  5. Repeat across all stitches. When all stitches have moved to the dominant needle, turn your work and start again.
Tip: If you knit every single row, the fabric you make is called Garter Stitch — it creates a bumpy, squishy texture that lies flat. Perfect for a first scarf or dishcloth!
4
The Purl Stitch

The Knit Stitch's Mirror

The purl stitch (abbreviated p) is essentially a knit stitch worked in reverse. Together, knit and purl stitches are the two building blocks of almost all knitting patterns.

How to Purl

  1. Move the yarn through the middle of your needles to the front of the project.
  2. Stick your dominant needle downwards to the front of your project.
  3. Do the rest of the stitch normally (wrap, pull through, slip off).

Knit + Purl Together

When you knit one row and purl the next row, alternating back and forth, the result is called Stockinette stitch — the classic smooth "V" fabric you see on T-shirts and store-bought sweaters.

Tip: If you are using a combination of knit and purl stitches, keep a pencil and paper handy to track which row you are on.
5
Binding Off

Finishing Your Work

Binding off (also called "casting off") secures your stitches so your knitting doesn't unravel. It creates the finished edge at the end of your project.

How to Cast Off

  1. Knit two stitches.
  2. Pull the bottom loop over the top loop and off your dominant needle.
  3. Knit one more stitch, and pull the bottom loop off again.
  4. Repeat until you have one loop left.
  5. When you have one loop left, loosen the loop, take it off your needle, cut the yarn, leaving about the same amount on the end, and pull the yarn through the last loop.
Tip: If you want to add more stitches, just cast-on, but if you want to remove them, knit through two stitches and slip them both off.
6
Reading a Basic Pattern

Pattern Abbreviations

Knitting patterns use shorthand so instructions stay concise. Here are the most common ones you'll see in beginner patterns:

AbbreviationMeaning
kknit
ppurl
cocast on
bobind off
st(s)stitch(es)
reprepeat
RS / WSright side / wrong side
yoyarn over (creates a hole / decorative increase)
k2togknit 2 stitches together (decrease)
pmplace marker

Understanding Gauge

Gauge tells you how many stitches and rows fit into a 4×4 inch square with specific yarn and needle size. Matching gauge ensures your finished piece is the right size. For practice swatches, don't worry about it — just knit!

Reading Repeats

When you see an asterisk * or brackets [ ], it means repeat those stitches. For example:

* k2, p2; rep from * to end means: knit 2, purl 2, knit 2, purl 2… all the way to the end of the row. That's 2×2 ribbing!

Your first project idea: Cast on 20 stitches of worsted weight yarn. Knit every row for about 8 inches. Bind off. You've made a swatch — or the beginning of a dishcloth! Aim for something small and rectangular to start.
