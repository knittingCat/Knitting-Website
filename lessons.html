Knitting Lessons

Six step-by-step lessons taking you from complete beginner to confident knitter. Click any lesson to expand it.

1
What You Need to Get Started

Your Essential Supplies

Before your first stitch, gather these basics. You don't need to spend much — budget options work perfectly for learning.

🪡
Knitting Needles

Size US 7–9 (4.5–5.5mm) straight needles. Wood or bamboo grip yarn better than metal — great for beginners.

🧶
Yarn

Choose a worsted weight (label will say "4" or "Worsted"). Light or medium colors are easier to see your stitches.

✂️
Scissors

Any small scissors for cutting yarn. Sharp tip scissors make it easiest.

🪢
Tapestry Needle

A blunt-tipped sewing needle with a big eye. Used to weave in your yarn ends when you finish a project.

Tip: For your very first project, pick yarn labeled "Easy" or "Beginner" and match the needle size printed on the label. A skein (ball) of 200–250 yards of worsted weight is plenty for a practice swatch or small scarf.
2
Casting On

What is Casting On?

Casting on creates the first row of loops on your needle. These loops become the foundation of your knitting. We'll use the Long-Tail Cast-On — it's stretchy, tidy, and the most popular method.

How to Long-Tail Cast On

  1. Make a slip knot about 1 inch per stitch you need (e.g., 20 stitches = ~20 inches from the end). Place it on your needle and pull gently snug — not tight.
  2. Hold the needle in your right hand. With your left hand, spread your thumb and index finger apart so yarn forms a "V" — tail yarn over thumb, working yarn over index finger.
  3. Insert the needle tip up through the loop around your thumb.
  4. Swing the needle over and grab the yarn sitting on your index finger, pulling it back through the thumb loop.
  5. Slip the thumb loop off your thumb and pull the tail yarn to snug the new stitch onto the needle.
  6. Spread your fingers into the "V" again and repeat from step 3 until you have the number of stitches you need.
Practice goal: Cast on 20 stitches without looking at your hands. It feels awkward at first — that's completely normal. Your tension will even out with practice. Try casting on and taking them back off until the motion feels natural.
3
The Knit Stitch

The Foundation of Knitting

The knit stitch (abbreviated k in patterns) is the most fundamental knitting movement. Learn this one stitch and you can already make a scarf!

A useful saying for remembering the steps: "In, Over, Through, Off"

How to Knit

  1. In: Insert the right needle tip into the first stitch on the left needle, going from left to right — the needles form an "X", right needle in front.
  2. Over: Wrap the working yarn (attached to the ball) counterclockwise around the right needle tip.
  3. Through: Pull the right needle — and the loop of yarn wrapped around it — back through the stitch toward you.
  4. Off: Slide the original stitch off the left needle. A new stitch now sits on the right needle.
  5. Repeat across all stitches. When all stitches have moved to the right needle, turn your work and start again with the full needle in your left hand.
Tip: If you knit every single row, the fabric you make is called Garter Stitch — it creates a bumpy, squishy texture that lies flat. Perfect for a first scarf or dishcloth!
4
The Purl Stitch

The Knit Stitch's Mirror

The purl stitch (abbreviated p) is essentially a knit stitch worked in reverse. Together, knit and purl stitches are the two building blocks of almost all knitting patterns.

The saying for purl: "In from the Right, Yarn in Front"

How to Purl

  1. Bring the working yarn to the front of your work (between the needles, toward you).
  2. Insert the right needle tip into the first stitch on the left needle, going from right to left — the right needle goes in front of the left needle.
  3. Wrap the working yarn counterclockwise around the right needle tip.
  4. Pull the right needle — and the yarn wrapped around it — back through the stitch, moving away from you.
  5. Slide the original stitch off the left needle. A new purl stitch sits on the right needle.

Knit + Purl Together

When you knit one row and purl the next row, alternating back and forth, the result is called Stockinette stitch — the classic smooth "V" fabric you see on T-shirts and store-bought sweaters.

Tip: The most common beginner mistake on purls is forgetting to bring the yarn to the front first. If your stitch count grows mysteriously, that's probably why!
5
Binding Off

Finishing Your Work

Binding off (also called "casting off") secures your stitches so your knitting doesn't unravel. It creates the finished edge at the end of your project.

How to Bind Off

  1. Knit the first 2 stitches normally — you now have 2 stitches on your right needle.
  2. Insert the left needle tip into the first (leftmost) stitch on the right needle.
  3. Lift that first stitch up and over the second stitch and off the right needle entirely. You now have 1 stitch on the right needle.
  4. Knit 1 more stitch. Again, 2 stitches on the right needle.
  5. Repeat steps 2–4 until 1 stitch remains on the right needle.
  6. Cut the yarn, leaving a 6-inch tail. Pull the tail through the last loop and tighten to secure.
  7. Thread the tail onto a tapestry needle and weave it into the back of your knitting to hide it.
Tip: Bind off loosely! Many beginners bind off too tight, creating a puckered edge. If your bound-off edge feels stiff or pulls inward, try using a needle one or two sizes larger just for the bind-off row.
6
Reading a Basic Pattern

Pattern Abbreviations

Knitting patterns use shorthand so instructions stay concise. Here are the most common ones you'll see in beginner patterns:

AbbreviationMeaning
kknit
ppurl
cocast on
bobind off
st(s)stitch(es)
reprepeat
RS / WSright side / wrong side
yoyarn over (creates a hole / decorative increase)
k2togknit 2 stitches together (decrease)
pmplace marker

Understanding Gauge

Gauge tells you how many stitches and rows fit into a 4×4 inch square with specific yarn and needle size. Matching gauge ensures your finished piece is the right size. For practice swatches, don't worry about it — just knit!

Reading Repeats

When you see an asterisk * or brackets [ ], it means repeat those stitches. For example:

* k2, p2; rep from * to end means: knit 2, purl 2, knit 2, purl 2… all the way to the end of the row. That's 2×2 ribbing!

Your first project idea: Cast on 20 stitches of worsted weight yarn. Knit every row for about 8 inches. Bind off. You've made a swatch — or the beginning of a dishcloth! Aim for something small and rectangular to start.
