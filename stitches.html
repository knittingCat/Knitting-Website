Reference Guide

Stitch Library

A visual guide to the most useful knitting stitches, from total beginner to first intermediate projects.

Garter Stitch
Beginner

The simplest knit fabric — made by knitting every single row. Creates a squishy, ridged texture that lies perfectly flat.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on any number of stitches.
  2. Knit every stitch of every row.
  3. That's it! Repeat until your piece is the length you want, then bind off.

Looks like: horizontal ridges on both sides. The same on the front and back — reversible!

Stockinette Stitch
Beginner

The most iconic knit fabric — smooth "V" shapes on the front, bumpy purl ridges on the back. Used in sweaters, hats, and socks.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on any number of stitches.
  2. Row 1 (Right Side): Knit all stitches.
  3. Row 2 (Wrong Side): Purl all stitches.
  4. Repeat rows 1 and 2 until desired length, then bind off.

Looks like: neat "V" shapes on the front (right side), horizontal bumps on the back. Note: edges will curl inward naturally.

Reverse Stockinette
Beginner

Stockinette shown with the bumpy purl side as the "right side." Used as a contrast texture alongside cables and other patterns.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on any number of stitches.
  2. Row 1 (Right Side): Purl all stitches.
  3. Row 2 (Wrong Side): Knit all stitches.
  4. Repeat rows 1 and 2, then bind off.

Looks like: horizontal purl bumps on the front. Identical structure to stockinette — just flipped.

1×1 Ribbing
Beginner

Alternating single knit and purl columns that create a stretchy, elastic fabric. Perfect for cuffs, neckbands, and hat brims.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on an even number of stitches.
  2. Every row: * k1, p1; repeat from * to end.
  3. Move yarn to back before knitting, to front before purling.
  4. Repeat this same row every row. Bind off loosely in pattern.

Looks like: neat vertical columns of knits and purls. Very stretchy — snaps back to shape after being stretched.

2×2 Ribbing
Beginner

Two knit, two purl columns alternating. Slightly less stretchy than 1×1 but with a bolder, more defined rib — popular for socks and sweater hems.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on a multiple of 4 stitches.
  2. Every row: * k2, p2; repeat from * to end.
  3. Remember to move yarn between needles when switching between knit and purl.
  4. Repeat every row. Bind off loosely in pattern.

Looks like: wider vertical columns of 2 knits and 2 purls. Classic, structured ribbing texture.

Seed Stitch
Intermediate

Alternating knit and purl stitches that deliberately don't align between rows, creating a pebbly, textured "seed" pattern. Flat and decorative.

How to Work It

  1. Cast on an odd number of stitches (e.g., 21).
  2. Every row: k1, * p1, k1; repeat from * to end.
  3. The pattern is the same every row — the trick is that when you see a knit stitch (V), you purl it, and when you see a purl bump, you knit it.
  4. Bind off in pattern.

Looks like: a bumpy, pebbly texture — the same on both sides. Lies flat with no curling. Great for borders and dishcloths.

Moss Stitch
Intermediate

Similar to seed stitch but over a 2-row repeat, creating a slightly more elongated, lozenge-like texture. Sometimes called "double seed stitch."

How to Work It

  1. Cast on an even number of stitches.
  2. Rows 1 & 2: * k1, p1; repeat from * to end.
  3. Rows 3 & 4: * p1, k1; repeat from * to end.
  4. Repeat these 4 rows for pattern. Bind off in pattern.

Looks like: a slightly larger, more textured version of seed stitch with small horizontal "checks." Reversible and non-curling.

Simple 4-St Cable
Intermediate

Your first cable! By crossing stitches over each other using a cable needle, you create a rope-like twisted column. The foundation of all cable patterns.

How to Work It

You need: a cable needle (cn). Work this cable panel over 4 stitches, with 2+ purl stitches on each side for definition.

  1. Work to your cable panel. Slip the first 2 stitches onto the cable needle and hold it in front of your work.
  2. Knit the next 2 stitches from the left needle normally.
  3. Knit the 2 stitches from the cable needle.
  4. This completes one cable cross (C4F). Work cable crosses every 4th or 6th row.

Looks like: a rope twisting to the left (front cross). Hold the cn in back for a right-twisting cable (C4B). The twist repeats up your fabric, creating a spiral effect.
